01 · Executive Summary

Investment Thesis

The Indian IT sector has corrected 22–25% in FY26 on fears that generative AI will displace traditional IT services. We believe this fear is overpriced in the near term and underestimates the Indian IT industry's capacity to re-platform itself. At 17.8x forward P/E — 16% below the 10-year average — the sector is pricing in a structural decline that the deal data does not yet support. The Nifty IT Index is a tactical Buy at current levels for a 3-year horizon and a strategic Accumulate for 10-year investors building positions through volatility.

02 · Investment Layers

The Three-Layer Framework

Layer 1 — The AI Transformation: Threat or Opportunity?

The central question hanging over Indian IT is whether generative AI accelerates or cannibalises the sector's revenue model. Our read is that the answer is both, at different time horizons. Near-term (FY26–FY28), AI deflation is real — HCL Tech's own CEO has modelled a 2–3% annual deflationary impact on services. Longer-term (FY28–FY35), the AI opportunity is vastly larger than the disruption. NASSCOM has identified a $300–400 billion incremental TAM by 2030, with AI services alone pegged at $10–12 billion in FY26 revenue — growing at 20%+ QoQ.

The key insight: Indian IT companies are not just consumers of AI disruption — they are becoming distributors of it. TCS crossed $2.3 billion in annualised AI revenues in Q4FY26. Infosys has an AI partnership with Anthropic. Persistent and Coforge are winning mid-market AI transformation mandates at rates that dwarf their Tier-1 peers. The sector is re-platforming faster than the market is pricing.

↑

AI Bull Case (FY28–35)

Indian IT becomes the implementation layer of the global AI stack. As global enterprises move from AI pilots to industrial-scale deployment, Indian IT firms — with their deep client relationships, change management expertise, and offshore delivery — are the natural execution partner. A $300–400B incremental TAM by 2030 with even 15% capture = $45–60B in new revenue for the sector.

↓

AI Bear Case (FY26–28)

AI-driven coding automation (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code) compresses effort-based billing. Headcount-linked revenue models face structural pricing pressure. Renewal contracts face 2–3% annual deflation as clients demand AI productivity pass-through. Revenue recovery from FY28-end onward; near-term earnings revisions remain a drag on multiples.

Layer 2 — US Macro Headwinds

The United States accounts for ~50–55% of Tier-1 Indian IT revenues. Three headwinds are active simultaneously: (1) A potential US slowdown — the Fed has trimmed its GDP growth forecast to 1.4–1.7%, the slowest pace ex-pandemic in a decade — which constrains discretionary IT budgets; (2) H-1B visa uncertainty and protectionist policy risk; and (3) the West Asia conflict creating broader risk-off sentiment that delays CIO decision cycles.

↓

US Headwind 1: Macro Slowdown

US GDP growth forecast cut to 1.4–1.7%. BFSI and retail IT budgets tighten first. Large outsourcing contracts face scrutiny. Discretionary spends (cloud modernisation, digital transformation) face delay. Historically, every 100 bps decline in US GDP growth = ~150 bps impact on Indian IT revenue growth.

↓

US Headwind 2: Visa & Policy Risk

H-1B visa restrictions raise onsite delivery costs. Any reversal in offshoring policy — while unlikely to be structural — creates near-term noise. However, companies have been building delivery centres in the US, Canada, and nearshore LatAm to hedge this risk. Wipro's Harman acquisition reflects this trend.

→

USD/INR: The Hidden Buffer

The rupee fell ~9.88% in FY26 — the steepest annual decline in 14 years — hitting record lows above ₹94/$. Every 1% rupee depreciation adds ~30–40 bps to EBIT margins sector-wide. This currency tailwind is currently offsetting ~200–250 bps of revenue headwinds in rupee-reported earnings, which explains why PAT growth (12%+) is diverging from USD revenue growth (5–6%).

↑

US Recovery Setup (FY27–28)

The Fed is in a cutting cycle. Lower rates historically trigger a 12–18 month lag in IT discretionary spend recovery. BFSI — still the largest vertical at ~32% of revenues — is showing early signs of revival. Deal pipelines for Tier-1 companies remain robust. TCS's $12B Q4 TCV is the strongest signal yet that enterprise conviction in technology investments is intact.

Layer 3 — India's Structural Tech Tailwinds

Beyond the US cycle, India's domestic technology tailwinds are creating a second growth engine that was almost entirely absent five years ago.

↑

GCC Boom: The $110B Opportunity

India's GCC count has grown from 1,700 in FY24 to 1,800+ and is projected to reach 2,400–2,500 by 2030, employing 3–4.5 million professionals. GCC revenues are forecast to hit $100–110B by 2030. Indian IT firms — especially Tier-2 — are significant beneficiaries as GCC setup, scaling, and transformation partners.

↑

Domestic IT Demand Outpacing Exports

For the first time in Indian IT history, domestic revenues are projected to grow faster than exports. NASSCOM projects domestic revenues at $58.2B in FY25, growing at 7% — double the export growth rate. India Stack (UPI, ONDC, OCEN, Account Aggregator) is driving a FinTech and BFSI transformation that feeds Indian IT service companies directly.

↑

ER&D: The Premium Growth Segment

Engineering, Research & Development exports from India are projected at $63B in FY26, growing faster than IT services. KPIT, LTTS, Tata Elxsi, and Persistent have significant ER&D exposure. The automotive electrification, semiconductor design, and aerospace digitisation cycles create a 10-year runway for ER&D-heavy Indian IT firms.

↑

Talent Cost Moat: Structural, Not Cyclical

India's cost-competitiveness advantage — operations 40% cheaper than Eastern Europe, with deeper talent density — is not a transitional advantage. India produces 1.5M+ engineering graduates annually. As AI raises the skill floor globally, India's talent pool is actually becoming more valuable, not less, for complex AI integration work.

Layer 4 — Valuation: A Decade-Low Entry

Nifty IT's forward P/E at 17.8x is 16% below its 10-year average. Reverse DCF models indicate the market is currently embedding terminal growth rates of 3–4% — a figure consistent with a structural decline scenario, not a recovery. We believe the correct terminal rate for Indian IT, given GCC, ER&D, and AI service tailwinds, is closer to 8–10%. The gap between embedded assumptions and fundamental trajectory is the source of the investment opportunity.

03 · Stock Selection

We have deliberately chosen across size and style — large-cap stability, mid-cap growth, and one ER&D specialist.

05 · Risk Framework

Risks to Monitor

AI Deflation Faster Than Expected

If GenAI compresses IT services pricing by 5–8% annually (vs modelled 2–3%), revenue deflation could materially delay the earnings recovery cycle to FY30+.

US Recession Risk

A hard landing in the US economy would trigger a second wave of IT budget freezes. Historically, a recession cuts Indian IT sector EPS by 15–20% in the first year.

West Asia Conflict Escalation

Direct revenue exposure is low (single digits), but a prolonged conflict could spike oil, raise global inflation, and delay the Fed's cutting cycle — compressing IT multiples.

Visa & Protectionism

Structural reversal of offshoring policy in the US or EU would raise onsite delivery costs by 40–60%, permanently compressing margins for large-cap firms.

Competition from Hyperscalers

AWS, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure are increasingly offering managed AI services that disintermediate Indian IT in cloud migration and AI deployment work.

Talent Attrition & Cost Inflation

Senior-level attrition remains elevated as AI-skilled professionals are poached by GCCs and startups. Wage inflation for AI talent could compress margins beyond guidance.

06 · Time Horizon Framework

How to Think About Each Horizon

3-Year Horizon (FY26–FY29) Near-term tactical

The primary driver is valuation re-rating from 17.8x to 22–24x fwd P/E, as FY27 deal ramp-ups convert to visible revenue growth and AI deflation fears are absorbed. Earnings recovery (10.8% EPS CAGR sector-wide) provides the fundamental underpinning. Target: Nifty IT at 46,000–52,000, implying +46–65% from current 31,400. Stock-specific catalysts: Infosys FY27 guidance (April 23), HCL Tech margin upgrade (April 21), TCS Q1FY27 deal ramp confirmation (July 2026).

10-Year Horizon (FY26–FY36) Structural compounder

The 10-year thesis rests on three structural pillars: (1) India's technology sector growing from $315B today to $800B–1T by FY35, driven by AI services, GCCs reaching $110B, and ER&D doubling; (2) Indian IT firms evolving from service providers to IP owners — owning AI models, platforms, and products that earn royalties and license revenue in addition to headcount-linked services; (3) India's demographic dividend providing 15+ years of talent cost advantage, reinforced by AI-human collaboration roles that India's engineering culture is uniquely suited to. At a 13% CAGR, Nifty IT reaches 1,10,000 by FY36 — a 3.5x from today. In an upside scenario (15% CAGR), it reaches 1,30,000. This is the compounding thesis for patient capital.
